Research Question: Which Factors in King County WA are Associated With High Crime?

Suppose that we are particularly interested in moving to King County WA, but want to know which cities or regions of this county are safest to live in. One logical way to deduce this information is to perform cluster analysis. In a nutshell, this method of analysis will allow us to group crime counts in King county data in such a way that help us to identify patterns and relationships between the frequency of certain crimes occurring and the population in King county in a given year. To do this, we first import our data.

STEP 2: Clean up the Data for Clustering

The next logical step after uploading the data is to clean it up before analysis. This intuitively means that we just aim to get rid of all samples that have missing information, or any variables that are not of the right form for clustering. To do this, we remove variables with word entries to ensure all variables in our data are numeric, and we remove als observations with at least one blank entry. In the Rescaling the data section below, we describe the importance of re-centering and scaling our observations so that they all lay within similar ranges in terms of minimum and maximum theft counts. This process involves first subtracting each crime count by the mean crime counts, and then dividing by the standard deviation of a variable (i.e.how much the given crime counts differ from their mean). Well, our binary variables created in STEP 0 only have values of 0 or 1, and hence have standard deviation of 0. Dividing by zero is illegal in math! Hence, we need to remove these binary variables from our data so we can center our data later on for clustering. This is our last clean-up step.

#remove text columns
cols_to_remove=c("county","Location")
KING_data=KING_data[,!(names(KING_data) %in% cols_to_remove)]

#convert variables to numeric
KING_data=sapply(KING_data,as.numeric)

#remove rows with at least one blank entry
KING_data=KING_data[complete.cases(KING_data),]

#remove binary county variables since they all have standard deviation=0
cols_to_remove=c("Grant","Grays Harbor", "King", "Pierce", "Yakima")
KING_data=KING_data[,!(colnames(KING_data) %in% cols_to_remove)]

#observe new number of King observations
dim(KING_data)[1]
## [1] 364

Notice that after all of this clean-up, we only lost two observations. This is a good thing! Its important to make sure that data left after cleaning is large enough to ensure we get reliable results in any analysis we do.

Step 3: Visualize How Correlated Certain Factors Are in King County

In order to visualize how correlated certain crime variables in our King county data are, we use a correlation coefficient matrix. The idea is that the darker a given cell in this matrix, the more correlated its two associated variables are. The color of the cells here are determined using a correlation coefficient. Simply put, this is just a number between -1 and +1 that tells us how much two variables have in common. For instance, a pair of variables Murder and Population for King county are highly correlated if their coefficient is near +1 or -1, and not very correlated if their coefficient is near 0. Now, you may be wondering - How do we know if high correlation is good or bad? Well, this answer depends on the context of the data youre using and the goal of your investigation. In this context, if we want to ensure we are moving to a WA county that is safest, we dont want Population and Murder counts to be highly correlated. On the other hand, say we enjoy city-life, so we want to move to a highly populated county that has lots of job opportunities. In this case, we would want Population and Jobs to be highly correlated (near +1).

Correlation Does Not Mean Causation

It is important to discuss a common misconception when exploring correlation between variables in data. Just because two variables are highly correlated, does NOT mean that an increase or decrease in one causes in increase or decrease in the other. In other words, two events can be related, but this does not necessarily mean that one event causes the other. For instance, raining causes one to get wet, while raining and wearing rain boots are just correlated. This is an important discrepancy to understand when analyzing our cluster results. Just because higher populations and higher robbery occurrences are related does not necessarily imply that larger populations cause more robberies in King County WA to occur.

STEP 5: Compute the Significance of Correlations

Now we know that we should keep these variables for clustering. The next step is to ensure that the correlations we are computing are significant. To do this, we need to compute a probability value, also called a p-value. This quantity essentially just tells us the probability that a given correlation coefficient is close to zero. Since we want our variables to be highly correlated, we want this probability to be low (close to 0).

#initialize matrix to store variables, their coefficients, and their p-values 
rows=c("Robbery","Robbery","Burglary","Population")
cols=c("Population","Burglary","Population","Murder")
KING_coeffs=KING_coeff_mat[rows,cols]

#convert to data frame
KING_table=data.frame(matrix(0, nrow = 4, ncol = 2))
colnames(KING_table)=c("Coefficient","p-value")
rownames(KING_table)=c("Robbery vs Population","Robbery vs Burglary","Burglary vs Population","Population vs Murder")

#compute p-values

KING_table["Population vs Murder","p-value"]=cor.test(KING_data[,"Population"],KING_data[,"Murder"])$p.value

The way cluster analysis works is by grouping data into groups, called clusters, where a given data point is assigned to one cluster if it its distance to the mean of that cluster is small.Because this process involves computing distances, we need to ensure that all variables in our data lay within close ranges. For instance, as is, the Population variable has a much higher maximum than the maximum of the Murder variable. This could make clustering less efficient. See an example of two variables with vastly different ranges in our data.

STEP 7: Choosing the Optimal Number of Clusters

How do we know the right number of clusters to group our King county observations into? This is a well-studied question. There are many methods to select the optimal choice, but we focus on the Elbow Method. This method involves computing whats called the Within Total Sum of Squares (wss). The wss is just the sum of all of the distances between each point in a cluster and the clusters center itself. If we add up the wss for all clusters, then this is the total wss. An elbow plot takes each number of clusters k and computes the total wss. We do this for increasing values of k, and plot the points. An ideal plot is shaped like an arm, where the elbow of the plot corresponds to the optimal number of clusters k. There are also a number of other methods used to identify optimal clusters in the case that there is no obvious elbow of this plot (cite here).

library(cluster) 
library(factoextra)

set.seed(123)
King_elbowplt=fviz_nbclust(KING_data_scaled, kmeans, method = "wss", k.max = 10) +

  theme_minimal() +
  geom_vline(xintercept = c(3,4,5), linetype = "dashed", color = "red")

The main goal of kmeans clustering is to make the distance between each cluster as large as possible, while making the distance between points and the center within each cluster as small as possible. As shown, when k=5, some clusters 2 and 5, while when k=3 and k=4, none of the clusters overlap. Because of this, we select 3 clusters. However, when there are 3 clusters, some points near the bottom of this green cluster appear to be far away from the center of that group. Hence, we choose 4 clusters as our optimal number.

Answering Our Research Question

Based on both our bar cluster charts and scatterplots, we can see that higher populations are clustered with greater crime counts for burglary, robbery, and murder. This indicates that larger populations are related to increased crimes in King counted. So, when choosing which cities in King County, WA to live in, this suggests that we choose those that are less populated, as these are associated with less crime based on our clustering and correlation results.
