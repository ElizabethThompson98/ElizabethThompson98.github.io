Using Linear Regression to Inform Public Safety: Which counties in WA are likely to contain the least amount of crime?

Suppose you are moving to, or living in WA, and you want to ensure that the location you live in is safe. Naturally, you’d want to ensure this means it has low crime rates. Perhaps you’re specific question is, which counties in WA are predicted to have the highest crime? Knowing this can help you navigate which counties you don’t want to live in. We can answer this question using a predictive modeling technique called linear regression. Before diving into this concept, lets first upload the data and get familiar with it! For this data, a given observation depicts the counts of different crime-types in each county of WA in a given year, among other variables such as population, city police department, percent of people living in that county, etc.

Before diving into the answer to our question, it is important to visualize our data to identify any observed relationships between certain variables and different crime types. Visualizing Our King County Model: Intercept & Slope

As a brief review, recall that lines are of the form \(y=mx+b\), where \(m\) is the slope of the line (change in \(y\) over change in \(x\)), \(b\) is its y-intercept (\(y\) value when \(x\) is 0), \(x\) is an input (i.e. number of assaults in King county), and \(y\) is the resulting output (i.e. number of thefts in King county). Hence, in our Kingmodel summary, we obtain an estimate of the y-intercept and slope of our model in blue above. That is, our model is of the form \(y=3.60248x+242.28270\), where \(x\)=number of assaults in King county and \(y\)=number of thefts in King county. Intuitively, this means that our model predicts that when there are zero assaults in King county, there are approximately 242 thefts, and for every 1 assault the number of thefts increases by approximately 4.

The values in the \(Pr(>|t|)\) blue box indicate the probability that these intercept and slope coefficients, i.e. Assault, is a not a significant predictor of theft counts in King county. These probabilities are called p-values, where small p-values (less than 0.05 typically) indicate significant predictors. Hence, in our model, Assault is a good predictor for the number of thefts in King county.

Assessing Model Fit: How Accurate is Our Line?

Let’s now visualize our model as a red line on top of our scatterplot for King county. This is an important step, as we must ensure that our model actually fits our data reasonably well.

#define our model for King county
  #input x: number of assaults
  #output y: predicted number of thefts
y=function(x){
  slope.estimate=3.60248
  intercept.estimate=242.28270
  return(slope.estimate*x+intercept.estimate)
}

#create data frame of inputs/outputs for our model
assaults=0:26000
thefts=y(assaults)
model=data.frame(
  x=assaults,
  y=thefts
)

#add our model to the original King county data 
KINGplot_with_model=KINGplot +
  geom_line(data = model, aes(x = assaults, y = thefts), color = "red")

KINGplot_with_model

Other than visual inspection, there are statistical ways to assess how accurate our model is at predicting theft counts given number of assaults in King county, i.e. how well our model fits the county data. We obtain this information from the Residuals and Multiple R-squared values boxed in red in our model summary above.

Residuals are just a way to visually observe the distance between our model predictions and the true theft counts given the number of assaults. We plot these residuals as points of the form (observed number of assaults, predicted number of thefts - observed number of thefts). The idea is that these points should all be roughly close to zero when we plot them, since ideally we’d want this difference to be small. See a plot below.

plot(Kingmodel$residuals,pch=16)

As shown, minus a few outliers, the majority of our distances are around zero. This is indicated by our Residuals distribution information in our first red box of our Kingmodel summary.

We also use these distances to compute what’s called an R-squared value. This is a value between 0 and 1, where values close to 1 indicate good model fit and values close to 0 indicate poor model fit. As shown in our second red box in our Kingmodel summary, our R-squared value is 0.981, indicating good model fit.

Comparing Influence of Assaults in Each County to Predicted Number of Thefts

This linear regression becomes interesting when we use it to compare the “safeness” of counties in WA. That is, we can compare the slope estimates of Assault as a predictor in each of the five counties of interest (Grant, Grays Harbor, King, Pierce, Yakima). We already computed these models, lets compare their coefficient estimates below.

#obtain slope estimates for Assault in each county

Kingslope=as.data.frame(summary(Kingmodel)$coefficients[2])
colnames(Kingslope)="King"

GraysHarborslope=as.data.frame(summary(GraysHarbormodel)$coefficients[2])
colnames(GraysHarborslope)="Grays Harbor"

Grantslope=as.data.frame(summary(Grantmodel)$coefficients[2])
colnames(Grantslope)="Grant"

Pierceslope=as.data.frame(summary(Piercemodel)$coefficients[2])
colnames(Pierceslope)="Pierce"

Yakimaslope=as.data.frame(summary(Yakimamodel)$coefficients[2])
colnames(Yakimaslope)="Yakima"

#combine into a table
slopeestimates=cbind(Grantslope,GraysHarborslope,Kingslope,Pierceslope,Yakimaslope)
slopeestimates
##      Grant Grays Harbor     King   Pierce   Yakima
## 1 2.591931     1.687175 3.602482 3.081643 2.043845

Intuitively, our results indicate that Grays Harbor theft counts increase the least for every increase in assaults, whereas King thefts increase the most. We can visualize these in a heat map below.

slopes=as.numeric(slopeestimates[1,]) #obtain list of slope estimates
counties=c("Grant", "Grays Harbor", "King", "Pierce", "Yakima")#define counties

#create matrix for heat map
heatmap_data=data.frame(
  County=counties,
  Slope=slopes
)

#visualize heatmap
ggplot(heatmap_data, aes(x = County, y = 1, fill = Slope)) +
  geom_tile() +
  scale_fill_gradient(low = "white", high = "black") +  
  labs(title = "Comparing the Influence of Assaults on Thefts", x = "County", y = "Slope Estimate") +
  theme_minimal() +
  theme(axis.text.x = element_text(angle = 45, hjust = 1))

As shown, the darker the color, the greater the influence that an increase in assaults has on increased theft in a given county. Based on these results in our heat map, it appears that the number of thefts in Grays Harbor increases the least given a unit increase in assaults. Hence, Grays Harbor could be the safest choice of all five counties when determining the influence that assaults in this county has on thefts. Keep in mind, that there are several other variables to explore. For instance, the population of a given county could also impact the number of thefts. This is just one example of the many methods for which we can use linear regression to inform public safety in WA.
